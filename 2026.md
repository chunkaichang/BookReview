## Jan-March
### Homo Deus
- Author: Yuval Noah Harari
- Labels: History :classical_building:, Philosophy :bulb:
- Review: Nice overview of how humanism emerges from the religion-only world and interplays with captitalism and romantic consumerism. The book then pivots on how humanism may collapse if homo sapiens are treated as organic computers, leading to a world where organisms are eventually controlled by a set of powerful algorithms. The author also points out an interesting series of transitios: from centralized data processing (religion) to distributed data processing (humanism) and then back to centralized computing (dataism).

### The Drunkard's Walk
- Author: Leonard Mlodinow
- Labels: Science 🔬, Philosophy :bulb:
- Review: Nice review of probability concepts with intriguing anecdotes of mathematicians and scientists. It also reminds me of the saying: "Luck is also part of skill." Though we are not able to control the success rate of Bernoulli trials, the number of trials is under our control. Would definitely recommend.

## April-June
### Your Inner Fish
- Author: Neil Shubin
- Labels: Science 🔬
- Review: This book is full of life-science trivia (e.g., I did not know hiccups are related to how tadpoles breath and the existence of Sonic hedgehog gene). Would recommend. 

### The Universe Within 
- Author: Neil Shubin
- Labels: Science 🔬
- Review: The book covers the history and various turning points of the Earth since the Big Bang. Concise but still very informative. Would recommend.

### The Book of Why: The New Science of Cause and Effect
- Author: Judea Pearl & Dana Mackenzie
- Labels: Science 🔬, History :classical_building:
- Review: Introduction to causal inference via causal models. There are some digressions, but I am looking forwards to read other more hands-on books on this topic.

### 臺灣漫遊錄 
- Author: 楊双子
- Labels: Novel, History :classical_building:
- Review: This novel is full of food and I learned some trivia from it (e.g., Calpis was already a popular drink in Taiwan as of 1930s). I like the turning point where one of the main characters found her blind spot and the novel closes the plot in an elegant way. It is interesting how the authors framed themselves as translators of a fictious novel written by one of the main characters. What a recursion! I would like to read the English translation in the future.
